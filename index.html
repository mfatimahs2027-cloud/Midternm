Reimagining Harlem Plaza

A redesign proposal focused on improving community experience, thermal comfort, movement, safety, and public activation.

Explore Project

Research & Context

Harlem Plaza (Montefiore Square) is a triangular public space in Hamilton Heights, West Harlem, bounded by Broadway, W 138th Street, and Hamilton Place. The 137th Street–City College 1-train stop is located at the southern edge, while City College sits one block northeast. The plaza underwent a major $18.4 million renovation in 2022, expanding its footprint and adding 36 new trees.

Despite these improvements, key issues remain. The central paved areas lack overhead shade, seating is limited to fixed granite walls, and there is little infrastructure for programming or events. With approximately 40,000 residents living within a 10-minute walk, the plaza has strong potential but currently functions more as a pass-through space than a destination.

Key Findings

Three major issues define the current experience. First, the lack of shade makes the plaza uncomfortable during summer, as newly planted trees will take years to mature. Second, fixed seating does not support flexibility or comfort for different users. Third, the absence of programming infrastructure limits activity and reduces the amount of time people spend in the space.

Users & Problems

Users

Local Residents: Hamilton Heights includes strong Dominican-American, West African, and African-American communities. Residents need a plaza that feels culturally grounded, with shaded seating, accessible design, and welcoming public space.

Commuters: The nearby subway station generates heavy pedestrian traffic. Commuters need clear circulation paths, better wayfinding, and improved comfort for short stays.

Small Business Owners: Local businesses would benefit from increased foot traffic and longer dwell times. A more active plaza could support vendors and economic activity.

Visitors: Visitors and students need a space that clearly communicates Harlem’s identity and feels distinct from generic urban plazas.

Core Problems

Safety: Conflicts between pedestrians and vehicles occur at multiple crossings.

Seating: Fixed seating limits comfort and flexibility.

Lighting: Poor evening lighting reduces safety and usability.

Activation: The plaza lacks programming and remains underused.

Final Redesign Proposal

The final proposal introduces three key interventions. First, tensile shade sails provide immediate relief from heat. Second, moveable café seating allows flexibility and encourages social interaction. Third, a cultural activation zone supports markets, events, and community gatherings.

Final Visual Concept

The final design combines shade, seating, planting, and activity into a unified vision for Harlem Plaza. It transforms the site from a circulation space into a community destination that supports daily use and cultural expression.

AI Reflection

This project used DALL·E to generate three concept images exploring different design directions. These visualizations helped test ideas quickly and compare approaches to improving comfort, seating, and activity.

AI was useful in visualizing possibilities that would be difficult to sketch quickly by hand. It allowed rapid iteration between greener, more social, and mixed-use plaza designs.

However, some AI outputs felt too generic or suburban and did not reflect the density or cultural identity of Harlem. This required filtering and refinement.

Human judgment was essential in shaping the final proposal. AI cannot fully understand cultural context, local identity, or community needs. Decisions about design direction and purpose relied on human interpretation and analysis.
